What the market is charging now
At the lower end of the market, the public price anchors are meaningfully below full-suite back-office systems. Backbar prices Essential at $79/month annually or $99 month-to-month, and Professional at $129 annually or $149 month-to-month, both per location. meez prices Starter Plus for a single-kitchen team at $79/$89, and its Scale tier at $179/$199. Optimum Control starts at $150/month plus a $250 setup fee. These products are either narrower than FnB Cost Pro or more legacy in delivery, but they establish the lower bound of what operators expect for single-location and early multi-unit tooling. 

In the middle of the market, MarketMan’s public pricing is especially useful because it is both current and transparent. Its Starter plan is $199/month and includes inventory, ordering, receiving, price tracking, and POS/accounting integrations. Its Growth plan is $249/month and adds automatic vendor management, waste tracking, unlimited AI-powered recipe creation, real-time recipe costing, and automatic COGS. For operators already inside the Square ecosystem, Square’s MarketMan-powered restaurant inventory add-on is $99 per location per month, but that price only applies on top of Square Plus or Premium and is limited to new MarketMan customers. 

At the premium end, MarginEdge now publicly lists $350 per location per month, with a $500 per location bundle when Freepour is included. MarginEdge explicitly uses “no surprises” positioning and includes unlimited invoices, unlimited bill pay, support, training, and no contracts in the base subscription. That makes it the clearest published “full suite” price anchor for a restaurant operator comparing modern back-office systems. 

For larger groups, the dominant pricing pattern is no longer transparent self-serve tiers; it is quote-led enterprise selling. Restaurant365, Crunchtime, Craftable, Apicbase, and WISK all route serious buyers into demos and custom quotes rather than fully public self-serve pricing. Apicbase’s Growth tier explicitly starts from five outlets, WISK says pricing is customized and can be quarterly, and Restaurant365 and Crunchtime both position themselves as scalable multi-location platforms with AI and operational breadth rather than commodity inventory apps. 

What the AI upgrade changes
Your new AI capabilities move FnB Cost Pro up-market because they attack the most painful restaurant setup and maintenance tasks: invoice entry, recipe creation, menu onboarding, receiving, and physical counts. That is exactly where competitors are putting premium value. MarketMan reserves AI recipe creation for Growth and AI ordering for higher packaging. Craftable markets AI invoice processing and automated 3-way-style controls as a core part of margin protection. Restaurant365 is now pushing AI-managed invoices, AI dashboards, and AI-backed accounting/inventory workflows. MarginEdge’s premium story is also fundamentally about turning photos and uploads into actionable cost data without manual entry. 

That means FnB Cost Pro should capture time-to-value and labor savings in its pricing, not just inventory or recipe management. Your AI setup scans also reduce the traditional implementation burden, which is strategically important because it lets you compete more like a modern SaaS product and less like legacy software. Optimum Control still uses a visible setup fee, while Square’s MarketMan add-on explicitly says there are no setup fees; your AI-assisted onboarding gives you a strong case for either no mandatory setup fee or only optional white-glove onboarding. 

Recommended pricing architecture
Starter — $149/month, or $129/month on annual billing.
This is the right place if Starter is limited to one location, three seats, and excludes shelf-scan vision, invoice auto-receive, transfers, and accounting sync. It places FnB Cost Pro above narrower tools like Backbar Essential and meez Starter Plus, but safely below MarketMan Starter at $199 and far below MarginEdge at $350. If you price Starter above roughly $159, you will start running into better-known products with broader published integrations and longer market credibility. 

Pro — $79/month platform fee + $149/store/month, or $69 + $129/store on annual billing.
This makes the first Pro location effectively $228/month, which is a strong position: it stays under MarketMan Growth at $249 and well under MarginEdge, but it monetizes the advanced AI/operations layer more appropriately than sub-$200 pricing would. By store count, the effective rate falls naturally as the group grows, which is exactly what a multi-location buyer expects from a base-plus-location model. This also aligns well with your own value story: more stores means more scans, more receiving, more transfers, more inventory events, and more savings captured. 

Enterprise — custom quote, with a real contract minimum.
Do not publish a lowball enterprise starting price. The market norm at that level is solution selling, not self-serve checkout. I would internally set an annual contract floor in the $15,000–$25,000 range, plus paid implementation for any multi-brand, franchise, custom integration, or advanced support requirement. That is directionally consistent with how Restaurant365, Crunchtime, Craftable, Apicbase, and WISK all move larger buyers into custom contracts. 

If you want an early-adopter incentive, keep it off the main pricing page and use it as a founder offer instead of lowering list price. A sensible founder promo would be Starter at $129 and Pro at $59 + $139/store for the first 25–50 accounts, but I would keep the public list prices at $149 and $79 + $149/store unless conversion data proves they are too high. Those list prices are the stronger long-term anchors against current competitors. 

Packaging and billing rules that will matter
Keep the current packaging logic where AI setup scans are in all tiers, but the more operationally powerful AI stays in Pro and above. That is the right pattern. Restaurants will understand that “faster setup” is foundational, while “AI shelf scan + catch-weight + invoice auto-receive + transfers + accounting sync” is a more advanced operating system. Competitors make the same distinction between foundational setup/value tools and higher-order automation. 

Your decision to keep transfers and accounting sync out of Starter is defensible, but only if Starter remains clearly cheaper. MarketMan already includes accounting integrations in Starter at $199, and both MarginEdge and Restaurant365 explicitly market transfers as multi-unit value. So the strategic logic is: Starter wins on affordability and speed of setup; Pro wins on operational control, finance integration, and multi-store workflows. If Starter drifts too close to $199, that distinction gets harder to defend. 

For Enterprise, “multi-brand/franchise” alone is not enough to justify a quote-only tier. The custom tier needs at least three contract-only elements beyond features: custom implementation, API/custom integrations, and higher-touch support/SLA or advanced admin controls. That is exactly how the enterprise leaders structure their packaging. MarketMan puts Open API in Enterprise, Apicbase reserves features like SSO/2FA and then adds SLA and custom integrations at higher levels, and Restaurant365 frames enterprise-scale value around flexible solution design across accounting, inventory, workforce, and payroll. 

Because your AI reduces onboarding labor, I would avoid mandatory setup fees on Starter and Pro. Instead, offer optional white-glove onboarding. A reasonable structure would be $299 for Starter, $999 for Pro multi-store onboarding, and scoped implementation for Enterprise. That lets self-serve buyers move fast while still creating a services revenue path for accounts that need more hand-holding. This compares favorably with the no-setup-fee posture in Square’s MarketMan add-on and the visible setup fees in Optimum Control. 

Final call
If this were my pricing page, I would publish it as: Starter — $149/month, Pro — starts at $228/month, Enterprise — custom. Under the hood, Pro would be billed in Stripe exactly as you described: one organization/platform subscription plus a quantity-based location line item. I would also market the AI as included, not metered, and use “optional white-glove onboarding” rather than mandatory setup fees. That structure is competitive, understandable, and better aligned with your upgraded product than a low-end single flat fee would be. 

One final caution: benchmark using official vendor pages whenever possible. Hospitality software pricing on review sites can lag badly. For example, an older marketplace listing still showed MarginEdge at $300, while MarginEdge’s own official pricing page now lists $350 per location per month. For your pricing decisions, the official current pages are the stronger signal.
